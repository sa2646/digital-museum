Samba Schools

The Heart of Carnival

Discover how samba schools prepare year-round and compete for the championship title, transforming from marginalized communities into cultural powerhouses.

The Birth of Samba Schools

The origins of samba schools are deeply connected to the history of Carnival in Rio itself. The first parades, or "cortumes," as Luso-Brazilians called them, were more exclusive events initially dominated by white revelers rather than the African-Brazilians who dominate today.

Samba players from a group called Estácio were the pioneers of present-day samba schools. The founding of "Deixa Falar" (Let Me Speak) in 1928 marked a pivotal moment. Ismael Silva emerged as a visionary, coming up with the idea of creating "blocos de Carnaval" where groups could dance and move to samba rhythms and sounds.

The Era of Competition

The first samba school contests began in 1928, held at the home of Zé Espinguela, where his Conjunto Oswaldo Cruz won. Mangueira and Deixa Falar also competed for the title. Some consider this as the starting point leading to the development of present-day samba schools.

Between 1930 and 1932, these were considered variations of the blocos (local street parade celebrations). In 1932, Mário Filho, owner of Mundo Sportivo, decided to sponsor the first Parade of Samba Schools in Praça Onze. Composers such as Antônio Nassar, Armando Reis, and Orestes Barbosa had the vision of organizing a Carnival parade supported by local newspapers.

Rise to Prominence

At the invitation of "Sports World," 19 schools attended the first major competition. The newspaper established criteria for judging participating schools, and news networks still have significant influence on the parades today, striving for fairness and high quality given the substantial investment in TV broadcasting rights.

The winning school was Mangueira, with Conjunto Osvaldo Cruz (Portela) taking second place. Officials ensured the contest's success remained at Praça XI Square in 1941. Over time, samba schools incorporated elements from ranches, such as the plot, the pair of mestre-sala (master-of-ceremony), and flag holders.

Modern Championship Competition

With the rise of populist dictator Getúlio Vargas and the foundation of the General Union of Samba Schools in 1934, samba schools gained power and importance in Rio Carnival, eventually supplanting the ranches carnival. The movement quickly expanded to other states, with the foundation of "Primeira São Paulo" (First of São Paulo) in 1935.

Today's competitions are tougher than ever. Samba schools are given 70-80 minutes to parade at the Sambadrome. Out of all participating schools, only 12 are selected to challenge each other in a two-day competition to win the coveted championship title.

Year-Round Preparation

Samba schools prepare year-round for their championship performances. Each school develops elaborate themes, costumes, and choreography that tell stories through music and movement. Performances embody themes based on arts, politics, or sports, all interpreted through harmonious choreography, groovy music, and upbeat carnival songs.

Beyond the thousands of performers who represent their school, countless members work behind the scenes to ensure their group will win the championship. From costume designers to musicians, from choreographers to logistics coordinators, each samba school functions as a professional organization dedicated to cultural excellence.

Cultural Heritage and Identity

Most samba schools are made up of members who come from the slums of Rio de Janeiro, but through these groups, the people of Rio find a sense of involvement and fraternity. Samba schools represent more than just Carnival – they're institutions that preserve Afro-Brazilian culture, teach history through performance, and provide community identity.

The traditional "ala das Baianas" (wing of Bahian women) remains a prerequisite to compete, and schools must present original sambas and meet strict requirements regarding instrumentation and performance standards. These traditions ensure that each performance maintains cultural authenticity while pushing artistic boundaries.
