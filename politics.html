Politics & Identity

Carnival as Social Reflection

Discover how Carnival reflects Brazilian culture, social issues, and national identity through powerful statements on race, justice, and community resistance.

Carnival and National Identity

By the beginning of the 20th century, Carnival found a strategic place in the delineation of culture and Brazilian national identity. Its ritual practices became bound up with the passionate features etched into the country and its people: its sensual tropical style and fondness for playful behavior and irreverent joyousness.

The sheer scale of its reach, spanning the entire country and crossing boundaries of class, race, gender, sexual orientation, and age, establishes Carnival as a national festival par excellence. It has created gestures, languages, and even codes, forming a plebeian cultural sphere where names and ideas can be exchanged, attracting diverse interests and allowing consensus to form—or stirring up conflicts in the face of clashing values.

From Class Division to Cultural Resistance

The middle class in Rio de Janeiro attempted to establish a single model for the festival between the mid-19th century and first half of the 20th century. They sought to model Rio's Carnival after the carnival revelries of Paris and Nice to "civilize" the Brazilian public.

While civilization did arrive to some degree—with parades of large allegorical floats featuring wealthy elite personalities—history intervened in this middle-class projection. The model was copied throughout the city, leading to unforeseen consequences such as the emergence of samba schools. These schools derive from communities who until the end of the 19th century were bound by the shackles of slavery.

Carnival revelries became significant in asserting the concept of a mixed-race nation and are intertwined with the idea and image of Brazil as a construct. These festivals have been acclaimed for their supposed ability to bring together distinct social groups in a playful manner, ritualizing values that allow communities to foster social identity while planning their character.

Platform for Political Expression

Carnival has a significant political and economic impact on Brazil. The celebrations fuel the country's economy, generating billions from tourism, hospitality, and entertainment while creating jobs for artists and boosting local businesses. More importantly, they serve as a powerful platform for political expression.

Samba schools use parades, floats, and music to critique injustice and amplify marginalized histories, making Carnival a vibrant platform for political dissent and cultural affirmation. Through bold song lyrics, provocative floats, and glittering costumes, samba schools pay tribute to the country's Black history while lambasting the racism and violence that Black Brazilians continue to face.

Confronting Racial Injustice and Police Brutality

More than half of Brazil's population is Black, but the country still struggles with racial inequality—a remnant of its painful colonial past. Brazil imported more enslaved Africans than any other country and was the last nation in the Americas to abolish slavery in 1888.

Police brutality is a severe problem in Brazil, especially in the favelas and low-income communities that are often stigmatized and marginalized. The statistics are alarming: in 2019, Rio de Janeiro police killed 1,810 people, averaging five people a day. Over 99% of all victims were men, nearly 30% were under 24 years old, and 75% of those killed by police in Rio are People of Colour.

Through their art, samba schools denounce violence and corruption while paying tribute to the victims of police brutality. These parades seek to draw attention, raise awareness, and demand justice for the victims, reflecting systematic violence and racial discrimination prevalent in Brazilian society.

Contemporary Social Themes

In recent years, there has been a fresh urgency to speak out against racial injustice as Brazil grapples with its own racial reckoning. The global outcry over the death of George Floyd reignited discussions about racism both abroad and in Brazil, leading samba schools to address these issues more directly in their performances.

Recent Carnival themes have addressed critical social issues: the Mangueira samba school highlighted the struggle for workers' rights and resistance to exploitation; Salgueiro addressed gender violence and denounced violence against women; Grande Rio explored indigenous Tupinambá culture; Portela told the saga of Kehinde, an African woman enslaved in Brazil; and Beija-Flor celebrated the history of popular Brazilian music from samba to funk.

These parades are not just celebrations but forms of protest and resistance. Through their art, the samba schools give voice to their communities' concerns, hopes, and dreams, reflecting the issues that matter most to Brazilian society.

Cultural Synthesis and Future Directions

Carnival is an innate expression of Brazil's people and culture, blending African, Indigenous, and Portuguese influences to celebrate Brazil's diversity through creative performances that empower communities. The festival temporarily inverts social hierarchies, abolishing existing divisions and transferring the "affectivity" of domestic ties to the impersonality of the streets.

In the last decade, there has been a considerable alteration in the "discursive order" regarding Carnival revelries in Brazil. Although specific national issues are not overlooked, the emphasis is increasingly on comprehensive scope because these celebrations were founded on basic values and aspirations of all humanity.

Nowadays, more diverse participants have taken to the streets, city squares, and avenues in different parts of Rio de Janeiro to enjoy Carnival. According to Rio's mayor, in 2013, five million revelers participated in the festival, divided into 450 groups of paraders, many called "blocks" and others "bands," demonstrating Carnival's enduring power as a mirror of Brazilian society and catalyst for social change.
